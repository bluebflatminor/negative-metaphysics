Negative
Metaphysics

Where the quantum-mysticism story breaks — and what survives once it does.

For anyone who has read the essays claiming physics vindicated the East, felt that a trick had been performed somewhere, and wanted to find the exact seam.

What the mathematics actually kills locality  ∧  separability  ∧  definite values

Not a worldview. A combination. Bell's theorem and Kochen–Specker strike out one package of classical assumptions and leave the rest of the field standing — plural, contested, and occupied at both ends at once.

Start here

There is a genre of essay that says quantum mechanics proved the mystics right: that Bell's theorem and entanglement confirm what Nāgārjuna and the Daoists already knew — a world with no separate substances, relational all the way down. It runs from Fritjof Capra's The Tao of Physics (1975) to its most serious recent instance, the physicist Carlo Rovelli's Helgoland, which devotes a chapter to Nāgārjuna. The genre is seductive, and its careful practitioners are careful. But most readers finish one with a faint conviction that a sleight of hand has occurred, and no way to say where.

This is an attempt to say where. The problem is not that the traditions are foolish; several are more rigorous than the essays that invoke them. The problem is a claim about what the physics does. Read closely, the mathematics does something narrower, and stranger, than the genre needs — and once you see the shape of it, you have a tool that works on every essay of this kind you will ever read.

01 — The move to watchSubtraction, not selection

Here is the whole hinge. A theorem in physics can do two very different jobs. It can select a picture of the world — hand you a positive account of what there is. Or it can subtract — rule out a combination of assumptions and tell you only what the world is not. Bell's theorem and the Kochen–Specker theorem do the second job. They are foreclosures.

What Bell forecloses is a conjunction: you cannot keep locality, separability, and observer-independent definite values all at once and still match what experiments show. Kochen–Specker adds that if you keep definite values at all, they cannot be non-contextual. Neither result tells you which assumption to drop. Neither hands you a replacement metaphysics. They partition the space of possible worldviews into a foreclosed region and a surviving region — and then fall silent.

The mysticism genre needs the first job. It needs physics to have selected a relational, non-substantial picture that the traditions can then be said to match. But physics only did the second job. This is the seam. Everything below is a consequence of it.

Two boundaries, so this isn't mistaken for something larger. First, on scope: the target here is a single inference — "physics confirms this metaphysics." It is not the claim that metaphysics must pass laboratory tests to be worth anything. Metaphysics has its own standards: coherence, explanatory reach, economy. Those are untouched. What fails is one specific bridge from an experiment to a worldview. (This leaves metaphysics not eliminated but constrained — a distinction developed at the close.) Second, on precision: Bell forecloses the conjunction given one further premise — measurement independence, roughly that what an experimenter chooses to measure is not itself pre-correlated with the system. Deny that premise (the "superdeterminist" option) and locality can be kept. It is a live position, rarely the one the mysticism essays are reaching for, but naming it is the difference between stating the theorem and overstating it.

02 — The most useful pictureLook who survives

If physics had really selected the relational picture, then the serious interpretations of quantum mechanics would converge on it. They do the opposite. Here is the surviving region, laid out from the most substance-committed account to the most relation-committed. Every one of them reproduces the experiments exactly.

◄ substance-committedrelation-committed ►
Bohmian mechanicssubstantival about position
Real particles with definite positions at every moment, riding a guiding wave. Deterministic. It keeps almost everything the classical picture wanted about matter — it just pays for survival in locality.
Collapse theoriesGRW / CSL
A small tweak to the dynamics makes outcomes definite on their own, no observer required. Substance-committed about the settled configuration.
Many worldsEverett
Take the equation literally; nothing collapses; the branches are all real. Determinism intact. No special role for mind or measurement.
Priority monismwhole before parts
The cosmos as a single whole is more fundamental than its parts — and entanglement is offered as an argument for this, not a mood. The disciplined cousin of "everything is one."
Structural realismrelations first
Individual objects with fixed intrinsic natures drop out; relations are what's basic. This is the rigorous version of the picture the mysticism essays reach for — and it's argued from the physics, not from scripture.
Relational QMproperties are relative
Rovelli's interpretation: a system's properties are definite only relative to another system, with no privileged observer and no absolute state. This is the survivor that explicitly reaches for Nāgārjuna — and the one we'll test the tool on in §04. It is also contested, one live option among the rest.

Every account on this line survives Bell. A view that keeps persisting, definite-position particles (Bohm) sits in the same surviving region as views that dissolve objects into relations (structural realism, relational QM). They cannot all be "the metaphysics quantum mechanics revealed." The survivors are plural, and they contradict each other.

This is the single most useful thing to carry away. The next time an essay tells you the quantum world is relational and process-like, remember that a fully deterministic theory of little particles with definite positions accounts for the same experiments. The physics did not pick a side. If a worldview claims the physics picked its side, that claim is doing work the physics never did.

03 — The deepest feature, and why it still doesn't settle thingsWhat non-separability does and doesn't buy

Give the strong reading its best card. Of everything quantum mechanics forecloses, non-separability is the one that feels less like a lost assumption and more like a positive discovery about the world: the state of a whole entangled system is genuinely not built up from the states of its parts. This is the real rupture, and it is where the generative readings get their traction. It deserves to be taken seriously rather than filed as one constraint among many.

But here is what taking it seriously actually shows. Non-separability is real — and it is realized in incompatible ways across the survivors. In Bohmian mechanics it lives in a guiding field defined over the configuration of the whole system. In structural realism it means relations are ontologically prior to relata. In priority monism it means the whole is prior to its parts. These are three different metaphysical homes for the same physical fact, and they cannot all be true.

Even the deepest quantum feature does not select a metaphysics. It relocates the disagreement one floor up — and leaves it just as unsettled.

04 — The tool you keepTwo things "resonance" can mean

When a tradition and a physical theory seem to rhyme, the feeling is real. The mistake is letting one word, resonance, carry two jobs that pull in opposite directions. Separate them and most of the confusion in this whole area dissolves.

Heuristic resonance
makes an idea easier to think
A framework you already hold can make a strange result feel familiar, give you vocabulary for it, even help you reach for the right hypothesis first. This is real and valuable. It is a fact about you and your conceptual equipment.
verdict → yes, this exists
Evidential resonance
gives reason to think it true
The physics confirms the tradition; the match is evidence the worldview is correct. This is the claim the genre needs — and it is the one that fails, because incompatible worldviews resonate equally well and none of them was ever at risk of being wrong.
verdict → no, this doesn't follow

Almost every quantum-mysticism essay works by starting you in the left column and quietly sliding you into the right. Yes, Madhyamaka can make an entangled world feel less alien to someone raised on it — that is heuristic, and it is fine to say so. It does not follow that the entangled world is evidence for Madhyamaka. The test that would make it evidence is one the tradition never took: it risked no prediction, so it passed no trial it could have failed. Compatibility with the results is cheap — nearly every survivor has it, including the ones the tradition contradicts.

Run the tool on the strongest case. When Rovelli reaches for Nāgārjuna in Helgoland, he is — to his credit — mostly staying in the left column: he calls emptiness a "philosophical home" for relational QM, a way of framing an interpretation he already had. That is heuristic resonance done honestly by a working physicist, and there is nothing wrong with it. The slide happens downstream, when a reader converts "this ancient framework helped a physicist think" into "the physics shows the framework was right." Hold the columns apart and you can see why that second step fails even here: relational QM is one contested survivor, and at the far end of the same axis sits Bohmian mechanics — same experiments, persisting particles with their positions intrinsically, precisely the self-standing existence Nāgārjuna denies. The physics that gives Rovelli his relational reading gives Bohm his substantival one. A congruence that also fits your opposite cannot be evidence for you.

Keep the two columns apart and you can enjoy the resonance honestly without being sold the conclusion. That single distinction is the most portable thing in this guide.

05 — Checking the technical claimThe logic that isn't the same logic

The most specific claim you'll meet is that the Buddhist catuṣkoṭi — the "four corners," where a proposition can be true, false, both, or neither — is the same non-classical logic as quantum logic. Both abandon plain true-or-false, so the identity feels safe. It isn't, and you can see why without a logic degree.

Catuṣkoṭi
adds more values
Formalized (by Graham Priest) as a logic with extra truth-values — both and neither join true and false. It relaxes the rule that every claim is exactly one of true/false.
breaks classicality by → multiplying values
Quantum logic
keeps values, drops a law
Every claim stays cleanly true or false. What changes is distribution — the everyday rule that "A and (B or C)" equals "(A and B) or (A and C)" — which fails for quantum propositions.
breaks classicality by → dropping distribution

They leave classical logic through different doors. One adds truth-values and keeps the laws; the other keeps two truth-values and drops a law. "Both are non-classical" is true and almost empty — so is intuitionistic logic, so are many systems. Being non-classical in different ways is not being the same. The strongest-sounding technical claim in the genre turns out to be its cleanest error.

(Scholars dispute how to read the four corners — some argue for a fully consistent reconstruction rather than Priest's contradiction-tolerant one. It doesn't matter for the point: on any reading offered, the four corners are not the non-distributive lattice of quantum logic.)

06 — A better home for a true idea"Everything is connected" belongs elsewhere

The Buddhist thought that nothing has independent, self-standing existence — that each thing is what it is only through its conditions — is a real and defensible idea. The essays pin it to quantum entanglement. That is the wrong shelf, and moving it to the right one is a gift to the idea, not an attack on it.

Dependence-on-conditions is a claim about what it takes for anything whatsoever to exist. Entanglement is a specific physical relation between particular systems. Mapping the first onto the second shrinks a completely general thesis down to one laboratory phenomenon. Contemporary philosophy already has the right shelf: the literature on grounding and ontological dependence, which studies exactly what it means for one thing to hold in virtue of another. Put the Buddhist claim there and it gets to talk to priority monism and structural realism as a peer — instead of being chained to a result about photons it was never about.

This relocation is more interesting because it is more accurate. The honest version of the idea is bigger than the quantum version, not smaller.

07 — The question worth askingWhy always these traditions?

There's a fair question underneath the whole genre, and it survives the demolition: why do the same traditions keep getting pulled into the quantum orbit — Madhyamaka, Daoism, process thought — and never, say, Aristotle? The honest answer is more interesting than "because they were right."

What quantum mechanics foreclosed was a specifically substantival package — separate things, with their own definite properties, sitting apart from one another. Any tradition that had already refused that package will, of course, look congruent with the wreckage afterward. It isn't prescience; it's that the foreclosed conjunction and the tradition happen to deny the same thing. And there is a plainer human factor: the people who go looking for these parallels are usually already fond of the traditions, so the search runs warm.

Not holding the doomed assumptions is something a Buddhist logician shares with a hidden-variable physicist. It cannot be what makes either of them right.

That last point is the quiet clincher. Bohmian mechanics also doesn't hold the full classical package — it's nonlocal, contextual, non-separable — and no one calls Bohm a mystic. Congruence-with-the-wreckage is common ground shared by views that contradict each other. Whatever is worth keeping in the traditions, it cannot be established by the fact that physics knocked down something they had also declined to hold.

08 — What's left when the story endsThe honest residue

Strip out the overreach and something real remains, small and worth having. Quantum mechanics did genuine metaphysical work — but negative work. It foreclosed a combination of classical commitments that many people had mistaken for plain common sense, and that is a serious result about the world. It just didn't replace them with a single successor, and it didn't crown any tradition.

The traditions keep their real dignity too, once they stop being asked to be physics. Their affinity with contemporary thought is at the level of stance — a situated, perspective-aware realism, as in Michela Massimi's work — and at the level of diagnosis: the old bivalent, substance-first picture was inadequate. They part ways at the level of specific cure. That is a truer and more durable relationship than "physics proved them right," which was always a loan against a bank that can't pay.

And notice the tell of a claim that's actually load-bearing: none of this needed Nāgārjuna to license it. The argument stands on the physics and the logic alone. When a conclusion still holds after you remove its most beautiful ornament, the ornament was decoration. That is exactly the test the genre never runs on itself.

09 — The wider lessonConstraint, not silence

Step back from the mysticism question entirely, because the subtractive reading points at something larger about how physics and metaphysics have come to sit together — and it is easy to mishear it as a counsel of despair. It isn't one.

Take the worry head-on. If the theorems rule combinations out without ruling a worldview in, and if incompatible ontologies all reproduce the same experiments, are we left saying nothing can be known about what the world is? No — and the difference is the whole point. What the survivors illustrate is underdetermination: the evidence presently available does not single out one ontology. That is not the skeptic's "we can never know." It is a specific, well-worn position in the philosophy of science — empirical equivalence among rival pictures — that says something local and precise, not something global and despairing. The corridor is narrow; it is not dark.

Nor does it mean physics is metaphysically inert. Physics has always done two things at once. It rules pictures out — the negative work this guide has traced — and it makes some surviving pictures far more attractive than others. Newton never proved absolute space; Einstein never proved that spacetime is a substance or a relation; quantum mechanics does not prove relational ontology. Yet each reshaped which positions stayed plausible. But notice what "more attractive" is: a pull on which picture to prefer, not a proof that it is true. It is the heuristic column from §04 again — now aimed at physics shaping plausibility rather than a tradition shaping familiarity. So metaphysics is not eliminated by the mathematics. It is constrained by it.

There is a historical rhyme worth hearing. Galileo and Newton did not abolish metaphysics; they changed where it could stand. Before them, teleology and authority and qualitative essence were legitimate participants in natural philosophy; after them, mathematical success became the court of appeal, and the questions moved after the equations instead of before them. Ontology did not vanish — it relocated. The quantum situation looks less like a second abolition than like a sharpening of that same arrangement. It is tempting to say physics has lately lost a positive metaphysical certainty it once had, but that overstates a clean before-and-after: Bohr and Einstein each held confident, opposite pictures, and the underdetermination sat under their feet the entire time. What changed is not that the world went quiet, but that we have gotten better at seeing how narrow the corridor of admissible ontologies really is, and how firmly the mathematics polices its walls.

Not "here is what reality is," but "here is what any acceptable reality must reproduce." Metaphysics survives — inside the corridor, not above it.

That is a subtler and more durable role than metaphysics held in earlier eras, and it is emphatically not silence. Inside the corridor there is still a live and consequential choice about which picture to build on, and that choice is where metaphysics does its real work: it steers which theories get looked for, which questions read as meaningful, which structures look promising. Einstein's realism was never experimentally required; neither is Rovelli's relationalism; both have guided research all the same. The walls are real and close. The room between them is where the thinking happens.

One last thing to watch for

Increasingly, the essays in this genre are assembled by language models — often several in a chain, each polishing the last. This makes the failure mode faster and harder to spot, because each pass raises the fluency without adding a single check. Several models agreeing is not several confirmations; if they share the same training and the same blind spots, their agreement is one blurry opinion wearing five coats. A specimen of exactly this circulated during the writing of this guide: a chain of five models produced a gorgeous version of the story that still misattributed a Heisenberg idea to Daoism, leaned on a physics proof known to be flawed, and called two different logics "precisely isomorphic" one sentence after disclaiming any identity. No pass caught any of it. The prose got better; nothing got truer.

The defense is the same tool as the rest of this guide, turned on the page in front of you: ask what claim is being made, and what would show it false. If nothing could, you are reading resonance dressed as evidence.

Note on one analogy held back. "Negative metaphysics" has a formal cousin in apophatic (negative) theology — both proceed by exclusion, saying what the subject cannot be rather than what it is. The resemblance is one of method only; the subject matter shares nothing, and in a guide whose whole purpose is to disarm seductive analogies it would be self-defeating to lead with one. It is recorded here, and kept off the page, on purpose.
